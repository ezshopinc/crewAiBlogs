Games Removed from the Olympics: An Informative Journey

The Olympic Games is a time-honored tradition that has been celebrated for over a century. What began as a small, ancient Greek competition has evolved into the largest and most prestigious sporting event in the world. With this evolution, the sports featured in the Olympic Games have also changed. 


Every four years, the International Olympic Committee (IOC) has the difficult task of selecting which sports will be featured in the upcoming Olympic Games. With so many sports played around the world, it is impossible to include them all. As a result, some sports have been removed from the Olympics, and others have been added. In this blog, we’ll discuss the history of the Olympic Games, the reasons behind the removal of sports from the Olympics, noteworthy sports that have been removed, the impact on athletes and fans, sports making a comeback, and the future of Olympic sports. 


Introduction to Olympic Games Evolution

The modern Olympic Games were first held in 1896 in Athens, Greece. Since then, the Olympic Games have been held every four years, with the exception of 1916, 1940, and 1944 when they were canceled due to World War I and World War II. In 1994, the IOC began alternating the Winter and Summer Olympics, so that the Games are now held every two years. The Winter Olympics feature sports like skiing and figure skating, while the Summer Olympics feature sports like swimming and track and field. 


What makes the Olympics so special is that it is a truly global competition. Athletes from all over the world come together to compete in the spirit of friendship and sportsmanship. However, with this inclusivity comes the challenge of selecting which sports to feature in the Games. The IOC must consider a variety of factors when selecting Olympic sports, including the history of the sport, the global appeal of the sport, and gender equity. 


Understanding the History of Olympic Sports

When you think of the Olympics, you likely think of sports like swimming, gymnastics, and track and field. These sports are the cornerstone of the modern Olympic Games and are featured in every Olympic Games. However, this has not always been the case. The history of Olympic sports is a long and complex one. The ancient Olympic Games featured sports like chariot racing, and the modern Olympic Games have featured sports like tug of war. 


When the modern Olympic Games were first held in 1896, the Games featured just 9 sports. However, within these sports, there were also multiple events. For example, the sport of athletics featured events like the 100m dash and the marathon. Over the years, the number of sports featured in the Olympic Games grew, reaching a peak of 28 sports in the 2000 Sydney Olympics. However, the number of sports featured in the Games was capped at 28, so in order to add new sports, the IOC would have to remove existing sports. 


Reasons Behind the Removal of Sports from the Olympics

The decision to remove a sport from the Olympic Games is not one that is taken lightly. The IOC must consider a variety of factors when deciding which sports to feature in the Games. Some of the factors that may contribute to the removal of a sport from the Olympics include a shift in the popularity and audience interest of the sport, logistical challenges and constraints, and regulatory and governance issues. 


Shift in Popularity and Audience Interest

One of the most common reasons that a sport may be removed from the Olympic Games is a shift in the popularity and audience interest of the sport. The IOC is tasked with selecting sports that have a global appeal. As a result, sports that are only popular in a few countries may be at risk of being removed from the Games. Additionally, the IOC must also consider the popularity of the sport with the audience. The Olympic Games is a global event that is watched by millions of people. If a sport is not popular with the audience, it may be at risk of being removed from the Games. 


Logistical Challenges and Constraints

Another factor that may contribute to the removal of a sport from the Olympic Games is logistical challenges and constraints. The IOC must consider the practicality of including a sport in the Games. This includes factors like the cost of building the necessary infrastructure, the availability of venues, and the number of athletes that participate in the sport. If a sport is too costly to include in the Games, or if there are not enough venues to accommodate the sport, it may be at risk of being removed from the Games. 


Regulatory and Governance Issues

Finally, regulatory and governance issues may also contribute to the removal of a sport from the Olympic Games. The IOC is committed to promoting the values of Olympism, which include excellence, respect, and friendship. As a result, sports that do not align with these values may be at risk of being removed from the Games. For example, sports that do not have a recognized international federation may be at risk of being removed from the Games. The IOC works closely with international federations to ensure that the sports featured in the Olympic Games are governed fairly and equitably.


Noteworthy Sports That Have Been Removed

Tug of War: Strength and Strategy

Tug of war is perhaps one of the most surprising sports to have been featured in the Olympic Games. Tug of war made its debut at the 1900 Summer Olympics in Paris, France. The sport was a part of the athletics program and featured both a men’s competition and a mixed competition. The men’s competition was contested by teams of 8 athletes, while the mixed competition was contested by teams of 5 to 8 athletes. Tug of war was also featured in the 1904, 1908, 1912, and 1920 Summer Olympics. 


So, why was tug of war removed from the Olympic Games? The answer lies in the shifting values of the Olympic Games. In the early 20th century, the Olympic Games placed a greater emphasis on the strength and power of the athletes. As a result, sports like tug of war, which were perceived to require strength and power, were featured in the Games. However, as the Games evolved, so too did the criteria for selecting which sports to feature. The IOC began to place a greater emphasis on including sports that promote skill, athleticism, and fair play, ultimately leading to the removal of tug of war from the Olympic Games. 


Baseball and Softball: America's Pastime Fades Away

Baseball and softball are two sports that have a long and complicated history with the Olympic Games. Baseball made its debut at the 1992 Summer Olympics in Barcelona, Spain, and was featured in the Games until 2008. Softball made its debut at the 1996 Summer Olympics in Atlanta, Georgia, and was also featured in the Games until 2008. The decision to remove baseball and softball from the Olympic Games was a controversial one, and was met with significant backlash from athletes and fans. 


The removal of baseball and softball from the Olympic Games can be attributed to a few different factors. One of the factors that contributed to the removal of the sports is the lack of global appeal. While baseball is a popular sport in many countries, it is not widely played outside of the Americas. Similarly, softball is primarily played in the United States and Japan. The IOC has made it clear that it is committed to including sports that have a global appeal, and that promote gender equity. As a result, sports that are not widely played around the world may be at risk of being removed from the Games. 


Cricket: A Global Sport with Limited Olympic Presence

Cricket is another sport that has a complicated history with the Olympic Games. Cricket was featured in the 1900 Summer Olympics in Paris, France. The competition was contested by just two teams, Great Britain and France, and was a part of the 1900 World's Fair. The event was a relatively small part of the overall Games, and as a result, cricket was not included in any subsequent Olympic Games. 


The exclusion of cricket from the Olympic Games has been a point of contention for many years. Cricket is a widely popular sport, with an estimated 2.5 billion fans worldwide. However, the sport has been unable to gain re-entry into the Olympic Games. The primary reason for this is the lack of an international governing body for the sport. The IOC requires sports to have a recognized international federation in order to be included in the Games. While the International Cricket Council (ICC) serves as the international governing body for the sport, it is not recognized by the IOC. 


Rugby: From Union to Sevens and Back

Rugby is a sport that has had a tumultuous relationship with the Olympic Games. Rugby made its debut at the 1900 Summer Olympics in Paris, France. However, the sport was only featured in one Olympic Games, and the competition was a chaotic one. The 1900 Olympic rugby competition was contested by just three teams, France, Great Britain, and a team from Germany. The competition was a part of the 1900 World's Fair, and as a result, many of the players were unaware that they were competing in the Olympic Games. The 1900 Olympic rugby competition is the smallest in the history of the modern Olympic Games, and as a result, rugby was removed from the Olympic Games. 


However, rugby made a historic comeback at the 2016 Summer Olympics in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. The 2016 Olympic rugby competition featured the rugby sevens format, which is a faster-paced and more high-scoring version of the sport. The inclusion of rugby sevens in the Olympic Games was a historic moment for the sport, and was met with excitement from athletes and fans. The 2016 Olympic rugby sevens competition was a huge success, and as a result, rugby sevens will also be featured in the 2020 and 2024 Summer Olympics. 


Lacrosse: Native American Roots to Olympic Exclusion

Lacrosse is a sport with deep historical and cultural significance to the Native American people. The sport of lacrosse was first featured in the Olympic Games in 1904, as a demonstration sport. The 1904 Olympic lacrosse competition was contested by just two teams, one from the United States and one from Canada, and was a relatively small part of the overall Games. The sport was also featured in the 1908 and 1928 Olympic Games, each time as a demonstration sport. Despite its limited presence in the Olympic Games, the inclusion of lacrosse in the Games was a significant moment for the sport, and helped to popularize the sport outside of North America. 


However, the sport of lacrosse has been unable to gain re-entry into the Olympic Games. The primary reason for this is the lack of a recognized international federation for the sport. The IOC requires sports to have a recognized international federation in order to be included in the Games. While the Federation of International Lacrosse (FIL) serves as the international governing body for the sport, it is not recognized by the IOC. As a result, lacrosse has been unable to gain re-entry into the Olympic Games.
